# Brazil

Brazil is one of the most ethnically and archivally complex countries in the Americas. Its genealogical record base reflects five centuries of Portuguese colonization, the largest forced migration of enslaved Africans in the hemisphere, and massive voluntary immigration waves from Europe and Asia in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Catholic parish records dominate the colonial period; civil registration became universal on January 1, 1889. Records are organized at the state and municipal level, with national collections increasingly digitized on FamilySearch in partnership with the Arquivo Nacional.

## Key Databases

### FamilySearch
- **URL**: https://www.familysearch.org/
- **Coverage**: The primary platform for Brazilian genealogy. Collections include Catholic parish records (batismos, casamentos, obitos), civil registration (nascimentos, casamentos, obitos), immigration records, slave registrations (1872 matricula), and census-type records. Coverage is uneven by state; São Paulo, Minas Gerais, and Rio de Janeiro are the most complete. Many collections are browse-only; indexed collections support name search.
- **Cost**: Free (account required)
- **Searchable**: Partially (indexed collections are name-searchable; many are image-only browse)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-searchable for indexed collections; browser-only for image collections

### Arquivo Nacional
- **URL**: https://www.gov.br/arquivonacional/
- **Coverage**: Brazil's federal archive, established 1838. Holds colonial-era documents, Empire records, early Republic records, land grants, military service, court records, slave trade documentation, and immigration records. Growing digitized collection accessible via the Sistema de Informações do Arquivo Nacional (SIAN). Cooperation with FamilySearch has made civil registration records available online.
- **Cost**: Free (free account required for SIAN)
- **Searchable**: Partially (SIAN catalog is searchable; some digitized collections)
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-readable

### Memorial do Imigrante (Museu da Imigração)
- **URL**: https://www.museudaimigracao.org.br/
- **Coverage**: Located in São Paulo. Holds the registers (registros de matrícula) of the Hospedaria dos Imigrantes, which processed over 2.5 million immigrants from the 1880s onward. Records include name, age, nationality, arrival date, ship, and destination. Also holds passenger list copies and correspondence. Covers Italian, Portuguese, Spanish, German, Japanese, and other immigrant groups.
- **Cost**: Free online access
- **Searchable**: Yes (online database searchable by name)
- **AI accessibility**: AI-readable (searchable interface)

### Arquivo Público do Estado de São Paulo (APESP)
- **URL**: https://www.arquivoestado.sp.gov.br/
- **Coverage**: State archive for São Paulo. Holds civil registration records, notarial records, land records, and provincial administration documents. Growing digitized collection. Particularly important for the large immigrant communities of interior São Paulo.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Partially
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-readable

### MyHeritage
- **URL**: https://www.myheritage.com/
- **Coverage**: Published 4.7 million Brazilian immigration records (2022). Also holds civil registration and church records. Good for DNA matching with Brazilian relatives.
- **Cost**: Subscription (some collections free)
- **Searchable**: Yes
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### Ancestry.com
- **URL**: https://www.ancestry.com/
- **Coverage**: Brazilian immigration records and some civil registration. Smaller collection than FamilySearch or MyHeritage for Brazil.
- **Cost**: Subscription
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (subscription required)

### Colégio Brasileiro de Genealogia (CBG)
- **URL**: https://cbg.org.br/
- **Coverage**: Professional genealogical society. Maintains a links database of Brazilian genealogy resources, online collections, and access guides. Useful for locating state and regional sources.
- **Cost**: Free
- **Searchable**: Directory and link index
- **AI accessibility**: AI-readable

### Geneanet
- **URL**: https://www.geneanet.org/
- **Coverage**: User-contributed family trees and records, including significant Brazilian collections. Useful for connecting with researchers working on the same families.
- **Cost**: Free (premium tier for some features)
- **Searchable**: Yes
- **AI accessibility**: Partially AI-searchable

### Hemeroteca Digital Brasileira (Biblioteca Nacional)
- **URL**: https://memoria.bn.gov.br/hdb/
- **Coverage**: Digitized Brazilian newspapers from the 18th century to the 1990s. Includes national titles (Jornal do Brasil, O Globo, Correio da Manhã) and regional papers (Diário de Pernambuco, A Provincia, O Liberal do Pará, etc.). Full-text OCR search across most titles.
- **Cost**: Free (no account required; CAPTCHA required since Oct 2025)
- **Searchable**: Yes (full-text OCR search within collections)
- **AI accessibility**: Browser-only (CAPTCHA required; see workflows/playwright-scraping.md)

## Record Types

### Catholic Parish Records (Registros Paroquiais)

The backbone of Brazilian genealogy before 1889. The Catholic Church recorded baptisms (batismos), marriages (casamentos), and burials (obitos) from the earliest colonial period in the 1500s. Coverage varies by region; northeastern Brazil and São Paulo have some of the oldest surviving records.

Parish records typically include:
- Full name of the individual and parents
- Godparents (padrinhos/madrinhas), who were often relatives
- Race or color designation (branco, pardo, preto, indigena, caboclo, mulato)
- Legitimacy status (filho legitimo, filho natural, exposito)
- For enslaved persons: name of owner, sometimes African nation of origin

FamilySearch holds the largest digitized collection of Brazilian parish records. Many are image-only and require browsing by parish. The FamilySearch wiki maintains a list of available collections by state.

### Civil Registration (Registro Civil)

Mandatory civil registration began January 1, 1889, following the proclamation of the Republic. The Catholic Church had performed this function under the Empire; from 1889 onward, records were kept at local civil registry offices (Cartórios de Registro Civil das Pessoas Naturais), organized by municipality.

Civil registration records include:
- **Nascimentos (births)**: Name, date, parents, grandparents, witnesses, place
- **Casamentos (marriages)**: Both parties, parents of both, witnesses, place, banns
- **Obitos (deaths)**: Name, date, cause, survivors, place

Civil registration records are held at local cartórios; many have been digitized on FamilySearch. For records younger than approximately 100 years, access may be restricted or require a formal request.

### Slave Registration (Matricula de Escravos, 1872)

The Lei do Ventre Livre (Law of the Free Womb, 1871) required registration of all enslaved persons in Brazil. The resulting 1872 matricula enumerated enslaved persons by name, age, color, occupation, nationality, and owner. This is one of the most genealogically valuable records for tracing Afro-Brazilian ancestry.

The matricula records are held at the Arquivo Nacional and are available through FamilySearch.

### Manumission Records (Cartas de Alforria)

Documents granting freedom to enslaved persons. Recorded at notarial offices (cartórios de notas). Include the freed person's name, the owner's name, conditions of freedom, and sometimes the person's African nation of origin or parents' names. Held at state archives and notarial offices; some digitized.

### Baptism Records for Enslaved Persons

Enslaved Africans and their Brazilian-born descendants were baptized in Catholic parishes. These records are often the earliest documentation of an enslaved individual by name. The quality of information varies: some records include the person's African nation (nação), parents' names, and godparents; others record only the owner's name with no personal information for the individual.

### Immigration Records

Brazil received major immigration waves from approximately 1875 to 1940, primarily from:
- Italy (largest group, concentrated in São Paulo and the south)
- Portugal (throughout the country)
- Germany (Rio Grande do Sul, Santa Catarina)
- Japan (São Paulo state; the largest Japanese diaspora outside Japan)
- Spain, Lebanon, Syria, Poland, and others

Key repositories:
- **Memorial do Imigrante (São Paulo)**: Hospedaria dos Imigrantes registers, passenger list copies
- **Arquivo Nacional (Rio de Janeiro)**: Ilha das Flores and Pinheiro reception center registers, 1875 to 1974
- **FamilySearch**: Digitized ship manifests and immigration records
- **MyHeritage**: 4.7 million Brazilian immigration records

### Notarial Records (Escrituras e Testamentos)

Wills (testamentos), land transfers, contracts, and manumissions recorded at cartórios de notas. These records often contain detailed family information and are held at state archives or local cartórios. Some colonial-era notarial records are at the Arquivo Nacional or state archives.

### Census-Type Records (Listas Nominativas and Maços de População)

Colonial and imperial-era population surveys conducted at the provincial or parish level. Not a uniform national census. Surviving lists are held at state archives. São Paulo has particularly well-preserved listas nominativas from the 18th and 19th centuries.

## State Archives

Each Brazilian state has its own public archive with varying digitization levels:

| Archive | State | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Arquivo Público do Estado de São Paulo (APESP) | São Paulo | Growing digitized collection; strong immigration records |
| Arquivo Público Mineiro (APM) | Minas Gerais | Rich colonial records; Minas was heavily settled in the 1700s |
| Arquivo Público do Estado do Rio de Janeiro (APERJ) | Rio de Janeiro | Imperial-era records; manumission and slave records |
| Arquivo Público do Estado do Rio Grande do Sul | Rio Grande do Sul | German and Italian immigration records |
| Arquivo Público do Estado de Santa Catarina | Santa Catarina | German immigration records |
| Arquivo Público do Estado da Bahia | Bahia | Oldest Portuguese settlement; strong colonial-era records |
| Arquivo Público do Estado do Pernambuco | Pernambuco | Important for colonial northeast; Dutch occupation records |

| Period | Primary Record Type | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Before 1889 | Catholic parish records | Church and State unified; church records are the legal vital records |
| 1889 onward | Cartório civil registration | Mandatory civil registration; church records continue but are not official |
| 1871 (one-time) | Matricula de escravos | National slave registration under Lei do Ventre Livre |
| 1888 | Post-abolition records | Freed persons may lack surnames in early records |

## Naming Conventions

### Portuguese Surnames

Brazilian naming follows Portuguese convention, which differs from Spanish:
- The order is typically mother's surname (first) then father's surname (second), the reverse of Spanish practice. This order was not always consistent historically.
- "Da," "de," "dos," "das," "do" are common prepositions linking surname elements and should not be treated as separate names.
- Women historically retained their birth surnames and may also adopt the husband's surname at marriage; records vary.
- Children born outside marriage may carry only one parent's surname or a different surname entirely.

### Surnames from Slavery

Enslaved persons often had no surname in records or were recorded with only a given name and the owner's name. After abolition (1888), freed persons and their descendants chose or were assigned surnames. Common patterns include:
- Adopting the former owner's surname
- Adopting a religious surname (Santos, Jesus, Cruz, Conceição)
- Adopting a geographic or occupational surname
- Retaining African-origin names as surnames in some communities

This means that the same surname may appear across unrelated families who had the same enslaver, and that the absence of a consistent surname does not indicate a single generation.

### Indigenous Names in Records

Indigenous persons in colonial records may appear with a Portuguese baptismal name, an indigenous name, or both. Colonial missions created Portuguese equivalents of indigenous names. The consistency of recorded names for the same individual varies.

## Search Strategy

1. **Determine the state and municipality** from family knowledge, immigration papers, or naturalization records. Brazilian records are highly localized; knowing the municipality is essential.
2. **Search FamilySearch** for Catholic parish records and civil registration for that municipality. This is the single largest source.
3. **For immigration ancestors**, search the Memorial do Imigrante database (São Paulo arrivals) and the Arquivo Nacional (other ports). FamilySearch and MyHeritage also hold immigration records.
4. **For Afro-Brazilian ancestors**, search the 1872 matricula on FamilySearch, manumission records at state archives, and baptism records from the relevant parish.
5. **For colonial-era research**, Torre do Tombo in Portugal (https://digitarq.arquivos.pt/) holds Brazilian records created before independence (1822), including parish records, colonial administration, and inquisition records.
6. **For state-level records** not on FamilySearch, contact or search the relevant state archive (APESP, APM, APERJ, etc.) directly.
7. **For notarial records** (wills, land transfers, manumissions), contact the local cartório de notas or the state archive where older records have been transferred.
8. **For newspaper mentions** (obituaries, birth and marriage notices, legal notices), search the Hemeroteca Digital Brasileira by surname and municipality.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming civil registration exists before 1889.** For most of Brazil's history, the Catholic Church was the only source of vital records. Civil registration begins January 1, 1889; before that date, only parish records exist.
- **Not knowing the municipality.** Brazilian records are organized by municipality. Searching at the state level without a specific town will miss most records. Narrowing to the correct parish or cartório is essential.
- **Confusing Portuguese and Spanish surname order.** Brazilian names typically place the mother's surname before the father's, the opposite of Spanish convention. A researcher expecting the Spanish pattern will misidentify the paternal line.
- **Overlooking the 1872 matricula for Afro-Brazilian research.** This national slave registration is one of the only sources that names enslaved individuals systematically and is available on FamilySearch.
- **Expecting surnames to be consistent across generations for post-abolition families.** Freed persons chose surnames in 1888 or later, and the choice was not necessarily inherited uniformly. Siblings may have different surnames.
- **Missing Torre do Tombo for colonial-era research.** Portuguese national archives hold Brazilian records from the colonial period, including parish records transcribed by colonial clergy and administrative documents. These are digitized and searchable.
- **Treating race designations as fixed categories.** As in Spanish colonial records, Brazilian racial designations (branco, pardo, preto, caboclo, mulato) were bureaucratic and social categories that could change across generations or even within a person's lifetime.
- **Assuming FamilySearch collections are complete or indexed.** Many Brazilian FamilySearch collections are image-only and require browsing by parish and year range. Always check whether a collection is fully indexed before concluding a person is absent.
- **Ignoring godparent (padrinho/madrinha) networks.** As in the rest of Latin America, godparents in Brazil were often relatives. Tracking godparent appearances across multiple baptism records can reveal family relationships not stated directly in any single document.
